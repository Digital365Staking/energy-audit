Building Physics

Building physics is the academic field that studies the physical principles governing the thermal, hygric (moisture-related), lighting, and acoustic behavior of buildings. It analyzes how heat, air, and moisture interact with materials and construction systems in order to optimize energy efficiency, durability, and indoor comfort.

Key Facts

  • Main areas: heat transfer, moisture, air, and energy.
  • Application: sustainable design and building performance.
  • Methods: computational simulation, material testing, in-situ monitoring.
  • Main objective: reduce energy consumption and improve thermal comfort.

Foundations and Scope

The discipline combines applied physics, civil engineering, and material science. It examines processes such as thermal conduction, convection, radiation, and vapor diffusion through building envelopes. This knowledge underpins energy efficiency standards and passive architectural design strategies such as insulation, natural ventilation, and solar control.

It shows a door fitted with a sealed red fabric covering with:

  • a large fan installed in the opening,
  • a measuring device (manometer/controller) mounted on the side,
  • cables and control instruments.

The principle of this device is to place the building under negative or positive pressure in order to measure air leaks. This allows the assessment of:

  • the air tightness of the building envelope,
  • unwanted air infiltration,
  • overall energy performance.

It is often used in new constructions or renovations to verify compliance with energy efficiency standards.


The image illustrates a comparison between water vapor diffusion and air leakage in a building wall assembly.

It shows a cross-section of a wall highlighting two distinct phenomena:

  • Vapor diffusion: refers to the slow and gradual movement of water vapor through wall materials, without any specific opening. This is a natural process driven by vapor pressure differences.
  • Air leakage (1/2 inch hole): represents a significant and localized air infiltration caused by an opening in the building envelope.

This illustration highlights an essential difference:

  • vapor diffusion is slow and distributed,
  • air leakage is fast, concentrated, and has a much greater impact on energy performance.

In practice, air tightness is a key factor in building energy performance, often more critical than vapor diffusion alone.


The HAM (Heat, Air, Moisture) analysis quantifies the simultaneous flows of energy and mass within the building envelope. This approach predicts condensation risks, thermal losses, and mold growth potential. HAM models are used to validate real-world performance under climate and usage conditions, and are essential in deep energy retrofits and nearly zero-energy buildings.

Sustainability Relevance

Building physics is a cornerstone of bioclimatic architecture and sustainability standards such as LEED or the Passivhaus Institute. Its application enables the integration of environmental performance, comfort, and indoor health throughout the entire building lifecycle—from design to operation and maintenance.

Research and Development

The field is evolving through digitalization, using dynamic simulations, smart sensors, and artificial intelligence to predict energy behavior. Academic institutions and research centers worldwide are continuously expanding its scope into areas such as climate change, urban resilience, and advanced functional materials.
