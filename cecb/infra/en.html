Building Envelope and Insulation in the Energy Sector

In the energy sector, a building’s envelope and insulation are key drivers of energy performance, as they determine the amount of energy required to maintain indoor thermal comfort. The building envelope includes all physical elements in contact with the exterior — walls, roof, floors, frames, glazing, and thermal bridges — and acts as a barrier between outdoor climate conditions and the indoor environment.

A well-designed and properly insulated envelope helps reduce energy losses, improve thermal comfort, and significantly lower heating and cooling needs.

Insulation consists of limiting heat transfer through these elements by reducing conduction, convection, and radiation phenomena. An efficient envelope therefore significantly reduces heat losses in winter, overheating in summer, as well as discomfort caused by cold surfaces or drafts, while lowering heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) requirements.

Diagnostic Tools

To analyze and optimize these performances, several diagnostic tools are used. Infrared thermal cameras allow visualization of surface temperature differences and help detect thermal bridges, insulation defects, or air leaks that are not visible to the naked eye.

The blower door test involves placing the building under negative or positive pressure using a calibrated fan to accurately measure uncontrolled air exchange rates and locate air leakage points.

Temperature, relative humidity, and pressure sensors are also used to monitor the hygrothermal behavior of building components over time and to verify comfort conditions and material durability.

Simulation and Optimization

In some cases, dynamic thermal simulation software complements these measurements to model overall building performance and evaluate different improvement scenarios. Together, these tools help identify weaknesses in the building envelope and implement targeted solutions to improve overall energy efficiency.
